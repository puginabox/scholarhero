Leadership in Action

The assignment was not a difficult one. We were assigned to work with the Boys and Girls club in Richmond, Indiana for a week. While there, we were to lead the students working in the positive alternatives program, a program for those who had been suspended or expelled from school, and we were also to reflect on the leadership we showed, as well as the leadership that those who we were working with showed. Fiedler's contingency model is an excellent model for the situation we started out with. Fiedler's theory states that there are three different types of variables that effect whether a leader will do a good job or not in any given situation. These three variables are relations, task structure, and position power. Situations with either bad relations, low task structure and low position power or situations with good relations, high task structure and high position power are better handled by leaders who are more task oriented, while situations between those two extremes are better handled by leaders who are more relationship oriented. Fiedler's theory, however, states that leaders are locked into one style, and that a leader is unable to change his style to suit the situation. Over the course of the week, I noticed the group using several different styles of leadership to match several different situations. This goes against Fiedler's theory. However, his model is still an excellent one to use when discussing the evolution of our project as the week continued. A better description of the way the group chose to approach the problem of what we needed to do is the path-goal theory. The path-goal theory states that leaders will use different styles of leadership in different situations and with different followers in order to achieve the best results for the followers. Using Fiedler's model, we were in a situation where we had bad relations, as the students were strangers to us, we had a low task structure, as at the beginning we really had no idea what we wanted to do, although our position power was high, as Brad told us to report any misbehaviors on the student's part to him. Therefore, according to Fiedler's theory, a task-oriented leader would have been the best leader to have during our initial situation. The situational approach states that effective leaders are able to “recognize what employees need and then adapt their style to suit those needs.” (Leadership, p.90) Although we were dealing with students rather than employees, this is exactly the mindset we approached Monday with. On Monday, we simply helped the students with their homework after introducing ourselves to them. Later in the afternoon, we assisted Aaron, a staff member working at the Boys and Girls club, as he did an activity. At this point, our leadership style could be described as supportive. We tried to assist the students doing their work, and assisted Aaron in doing his activity, but going with the advice that Brad had given us earlier, we didn't engage in any directive behavior, as many of the students were distrustful of authority figures. As the week wore on, we shifted from a supportive leadership style to a coaching leadership style, encouraging many of them to do their homework, as well as to participate in the activities that we set out for them. An excellent example of leadership we saw throughout the week was Aaron, a staff member working at the Boys and Girls club. Aaron is in charge of conflict resolution, which involves him leading activities that the students all participate in. Aaron frequently uses the path-goal theory, which is to say that every day, he has a goal, and realizes that every day, the students will be in a different situation. Some may be in a bad mood that day and not feel like participating, others will feel excited and want to participate. Aaron realizes that these scenarios change each day, and is prepared to make accommodations for the students. For instance, if he realizes that some students are in a bad mood that day, he won't force them to participate in the activity. However, if the student refuses to participate, Aaron may ask them why they're not participating, or use their refusal to participate as an example, or to elaborate on a point that he wants to make. He will change his behavior to suit the needs of the students for that day, and act accordingly. The entire focus of the program is about transformational leadership. The students come to the program having been expelled or suspended from the school that they were a part of. The point of the program is that it will give them a “positive alternative,” or show them that what they did in school doesn't have to happen again, and that they don't have to keep making the same mistakes throughout their life. The idea is to transform them, to increase their motivation to perform well in school, as well as to avoid conflict, and to avoid making the same choices that landed them there in the first place, in theory increasing their morality, as some students ended up there by stealing or by harming others. Ethics of those facilitating the program are very important. The type of service that is performed by these facilitators for the Boys and Girls club is by nature altruistic. It is very difficult to justify dealing with troubled children on a day to day basis if you're in it for yourself or to make yourself look good. Additionally, it is very important for the facilitators of the program to have a strong ethic themselves. If they're perceived as corrupt by the students they serve, the leadership that the facilitators are able to exhort over the students lessens, and the program as a whole is less effective. If I take away nothing else from my experiences with the Boys and Girls Club, I feel that it's important to take away this: Not every theory will work in every situation, and not every approach is suited for every situation. Fiedler's theory is great for looking at situations and what sort of leadership will work within them, but breaks down when you take into account that leaders can change their style and what they're focusing on to better encompass more situations. The situational approach is great for describing how a leader and followers may interact, but doesn't take into account outside influences and things like task structure. Path-goal theory is extremely complex, and somewhat difficult to definitively pin down. Transformational leadership can be somewhat nebulous at times, encompassing a huge variety of different situations and emotions. Bits and pieces of various theories will fit into different situations perfectly, but there will almost never be a complete match. .
